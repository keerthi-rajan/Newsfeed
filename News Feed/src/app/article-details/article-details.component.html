Doctors Are Called to Heal, Not Just to Treat

117 Comments !

As a doctor for the past 17 years, I've had the privilege of caring for patients. I'm welcomed into their lives at one of the hardest moments: a moment of uncertainty. A moment of fear. A moment of overwhelming tumult. I've realized over the years, and more so as technology progresses, that the humanity of medicine is at an impasse.

I see new patients all the time. Recently, a newly diagnosed 44-year-old breast cancer patient was coming to me for a second opinion and had traveled from several states away. Having already seen her, my oncology fellow shared the patient's stats, including biopsy report, scans and blood tests. I asked, "Why is she here?" A fellow restated, "She has a neglected breast cancer. It is locally advanced."

I paused. "Thank you for giving me an excellent picture of her cancer," I said, "but what is her real story?" Scans, stats and blood work only provide a fraction of the picture needed to treat cancer patients.

I took a different approach. As I walked into the room, I noticed the patient wouldn't make eye contact with me. I thanked her for coming to see me while shaking her hand. Still shy, she looked up, mentioning her discomfort with city traffic and parking. I pulled my chair close to her and asked what she did for a living.

She opened up and told me she and her husband owned a plumbing business. Eight months back, he was diagnosed with metastatic pancreatic cancer. She spent every waking moment of the last eight months caring for him. He passed away four weeks prior to our meeting. She felt the lump in her breast prior to her husband's diagnosis, but when she got the courage to see a doctor, he fell ill, and his health took precedence over her own. I got the full story – something my fellow was unable to ascertain from blood tests and lab results.

Science, advancing at an accelerated pace, is constantly challenging the medical profession, presenting new options and providing new treatments. While innovation remains crucial to the practice of medicine, physicians cannot forget our humanity. In order to provide guidance and hope – and sometimes, unfortunately, devastating news – we have to listen.

An alarming statistic was recently published that said doctors listen to their patients for 11 seconds on average. I have the honor to work in a multidisciplinary center with the latest technology and an advanced team at my fingertips. In theory, I could glean everything I need from a patient's electronic medical record and provide a recommended course of treatment. But I don't. I don't walk into a patient's room and know what they need, the course of treatment I will prescribe or the best actions to take. I don't know because I don't know them yet. And that is the point: Listen to your patient. Know your patient.

It's important to me to get to know each person – not each diagnosis. I want to learn their hopes and dreams, understand their fears and learn about their support system. Through the course of listening and knowing my patient, only then can I come up with an appropriate course of action for their individual needs. I went to medical school, and I have years of experience. But I'm not my patient. I need, each day with each patient, to be more than the white coat and more than the stethoscope.

Science is imperative. Medical research is essential. We make breakthroughs every day. But we need to do better than 11 seconds for each patient. I understand the power that science and technology bring, but I will never forget the simplistic power of communication and the lifesaving impact it has for the patients I see.
